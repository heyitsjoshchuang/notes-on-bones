Your First Click

2022-08-18
2022-08-18
4分鐘閱讀

The bones can be played in a variety of ways, each with its own strengths and quirks. In this page, we will learn the basics of how the bones work and how you can play your first click.

Grip

The most common way to hold the bones is to have a single pair in the hand. As for where in the hand to put the bones, one way is to slot one bone between the base of the index and middle fingers, and the other between the middle and ring fingers. The two bones should curve away from each other. Curve the fingers at the base and middle joint as if you are making a fist, and the middle and ring fingers lay on the side of the bones. You can see it demonstrated by Graham Hargrove here [1] . For consistency, this is the grip we will use throughout this wiki.

Quick note on timbre: if you need to play rather quietly, have the bones poke out on top around 1/3 of its length, and it will make a duller, less piercing sound. Also, if one end of your bone is thicker than the other, it will sound different depending on which end is up.

To play the bones, the one between the index and middle fingers is pinned to the palm with your middle finger. It should not move with respect to the palm, hence we will refer to it as the fixed bone. The other one is allowed to pivot around the middle and ring finger, hence we will refer to it as the free bone.

In The Rudiments of Rhythm Bones [2] , the terms stationary and moving are used.

Movement

The bones make a click when the free bone strikes the fixed bone, so keep reminding yourself to keep the fixed bone fixed! The free bone may tend to slip out of the hand, but it’ll stop happening as your movements become more efficient.

Your first click: the tap

Now let’s learn our first basic move: the tap, which makes a single click. Start with the hand more or less upright, then apply a pulse of force to rotate it away from you, ending about 60 to 90 degrees forward, as demonstrated by Hans Weehuizen here [3] .

Physically, this motion is analogous to flicking water off your hand: the stopping at the end of the motion allows the water to fly off due to its own inertia. The same priciple applies here, allowing the free bone to keep rotating even when your hand and the fixed bone have stopped, resulting in the two striking together.

Getting a clean tap

You may find that the bones click when you don’t expect them to. Don’t worry, as those extra clicks are the precursors to many advanced moves that allows you to play multiple notes in quick succession. For now, though, we do want to clean up the tap first, and there are basically two things you can do:

  1. Move the base of the ring finger to manipulate the free bone directly.
  2. Control the movement of the hand to manipulate the relative position between the free and fixed bone.

Both are important for different reasons: The clamping strength of the middle and ring fingers determines how fast the free bone rebounds, and the angle of the hand largely dictates which moves can and cannot be initiated. Here I would argue that, in order to not be limited in the moves available, you should not rely on pinching with the ring finger to eliminate unwanted clicks, but instead focus on developing control over your hand position. We will get back to ring finger control when we learn how to play at different speeds.

If you are getting more than one click when flicking forward, your ring finger is probably too tight. Try playing with just enough pressure to keep the free bone from flying off.

If you are getting an extra click when rotating your hand back, make sure you are only doing a backward rotation, not a downward movement. This is the starting motion of the forward duplet, which you can get a preview of here [4] , as demonstrated by Kafari. Another thing to try out is to not rotate the hand all the way back to upright. If we rotate too far back, we may trigger the backward duplet, demontrated in another video [5] by Kafari.
