Tap

2022-08-22
2022-08-22
3 min read

The tap is a move that makes a single click. It is part of the triplet, the forward duplet, the upside-down duplet, and the quadruplet.

Movement

The tap can be initiated from a wide range of hand angles, from upright to rotated nearly 180 degrees forward. A sharp snapping foward rotation is applied, causing the free bone to flicked towards the fixed bone, making a single click. Depending on the sharpness of the rotation, the end position can be more than 90 degrees forward from the beginning position for a soft rotaion, or within 30 degrees for an explosive rotaion.

Physically, this motion is analogous to flicking water off your hand. When flicking water off, the stop at the end of the motion allows the water to fly off due to its own inertia. The same priciple applies here, allowing the free bone to keep rotating even when your hand and the fixed bone have stopped, resulting in the two striking together.

To play louder, throw the hand forward and press with the ring finger to add momentum. The tap can alternatively be performed completely with the ring finger, without rotation from the hand.

To play taps consecutively, time the movement with the springing motion of the free bone. Apply the next rotation when the free bone rebounds to the farthest point from the fixed bone. Or, wait until the residual momentum of the free bone damps out. When playing only with the ring finger, press the free bone against the fixed bone to stop the free bone immediately. Note that this results in a closed, duller tone.

Usage

Used when a single click is desired. Can be used as the only move when the notes are sparse.

Troubleshooting

If you are getting extra clicks following the main click, try playing with less ring finger pressure to reduce bouncing.

If you are getting an extra click before the main click, you might be moving the hand forward or downward before the rotation. The extra click can be part of the forward duplet, or the beginning of the backward duplet.

Progression

Start with the hand upright and end 60 to 90 degrees forward. As the forward rotation becomes sharper, it becomes possible to perform the tap with minimal motion. The tap is most likely the first move you learn.
